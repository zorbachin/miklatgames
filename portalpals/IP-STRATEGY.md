# 🌀 PORTAL PALS — franchise IP strategy v2

**Source property:** *ZERO DAY*, feature screenplay by Zorba Grashin & Joe
Harkins — a sci-fi action-comedy about Max Shifrin, a nerdy PortalTech
genius who discovers he's a human/Parabot hybrid, "the Key" built to open
and close the portals between Earth and the Parabot world, and the cyborg
Cybersquad who drag him into saving both planets.

**The brand is already in the script.** When Max joins the squad he
declares *"We're all Portal Pals now!"* and captions his selfie with Harper
**PORTAL PALS**. The kids' franchise name is diegetic — the adult movie
hands the kid brand its title on page 37. That's the opportunity: build
**Portal Pals**, the animated kids' universe of *Zero Day*, as a game-first
IP that grows into series, film, merchandise, and location-based
entertainment — while the *Zero Day* feature remains the future grown-up
tentpole (the Transformers / Rescue Bots dual-track, run in the right
order: kids' brand first, because it's cheaper to validate and builds the
audience the tentpole later monetizes).

**Operating rule:** rigor and spend scale with validation. Every phase must
(a) stand alone as a win and (b) produce the audience + data that de-risks
and finances the next phase. No phase is greenlit on faith.

---

## 0 · What the script gives us (the franchise audit)

1. **A five-hero ensemble with built-in play verbs.** The script's squad
   maps straight onto distinct gameplay mechanics and toy identities —
   no invention required (see §0.5).
2. **Modular cyborg gear = the toy system.** The Cybersquad swap cartridges
   and orbs into their bodies mid-fight: Collins' blue ice-web cartridge,
   the green power tubes, the blue leap-frog orbs, Harper's arm cannon.
   Swappable, collectible accessory packs are *canon*.
3. **Vehicles.** The LASS — "Land, Air, Sea, and if you need, Space" — is
   a ready-made anchor playset/vehicle line.
4. **Infinite worlds through portals.** Portals to Galaxy X-16 (and the
   script's hundreds of rainbow mini-portals) give the franchise unbounded
   settings for seasons, levels, toy waves, and park lands.
5. **A redeemable enemy.** The script's own dueling narrators establish the
   Parabots were *peaceful* until attacked — they built portals to escape.
   For the kids' brand this is gold: Parabots aren't monsters, they're
   misunderstood; some are friends. Max being both human and Parabot makes
   the hero the bridge between worlds — an identity story kids attach to.
6. **A shapeshifting villain with a henchbot.** Dorsey (secretly a Parabot,
   "playing you like a fiddle") and Deb-bot are a classic kids-cartoon
   villain duo as written.
7. **Comedy engine.** The Strong Voice vs. Nerdy Voice bickering narration
   that opens the script is already a kids'-cartoon framing device.
8. **The ending is the brand thesis.** Max's *algorithm* wins the day — and
   in the final reversal the nerd saves the soldier (Max rescues Harper)
   and gets the girl. Brains-beat-brawn, delivered by the least likely
   hero, is the message every episode reprises: the smallest Pal with the
   smartest idea seals the portal. And the script closes on an
   "adventure continues" beat — the sequel/series hook is native to the
   property, not bolted on.

## 0.5 · The Portal Pals adaptation (character layer → play layer)

Kid-ify the cast (younger, brighter, zero gore — Parabots get "powered
down," never killed). Each Pal's signature move comes straight from the
script and becomes a game mechanic and a toy feature:

| Pal | From the script | Play verb | Toy identity |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Max** — the Key | birthmark opens/closes portals, master coder | open/close portals, hack (timing/puzzle) | glowing-wrist hero figure; the emblem kids wear |
| **Harper** — the Colonel | arm cannon, fearless leader | aim & blast | swappable arm-cannon figure |
| **Collins** | palm-blast strength, ice-web cartridge | smash & freeze | cartridge-swap action figure |
| **Rizzo** | corkscrew rocket flight, 360° spin | fly & dodge | spinning-flight figure |
| **Wu** | pops in/out of portals, tongue-click | portal-hop trickster (teleport/sequence) | pop-through-portal gimmick figure |

Supporting canon: **Charlene** (shotgun-competent, her coffee shop "Brew Me
Like You Should" survives every blast — the team's home base in the kids'
show), **President McCarthy** (comedy authority), **Maxine** (mom, cookies,
heart), **Dorsey + Deb-bot** (villains).

**Mascot slot (gate 1 decision):** a small friendly Parabot companion — the
plush/icon that doesn't age. The script's opening tragically loses a little
Parabot; the kids' universe rescues that character type and makes it the
brand's Pikachu. Non-verbal, expressive, lives in every scene transition.

Canon lives in **one database** — characters, gadget cartridges, portal
worlds, Parabot types, episode events. Game, shorts, series, toys, and park
scenes are all renderers of the same data. That single source of truth is
the franchise's real asset.

## The phase ladder

Each phase: **the win it must be on its own → what it proves → the gate
that unlocks the next.** Gate numbers are directional; lock them against
current category benchmarks at each greenlight.

### PHASE 1 — Secure & seed (weeks, not months)

- **Chain of title.** Lock rights to the *Zero Day* screenplay and all
  derivative works — worldwide, all media, both tracks (kids' *Portal
  Pals* and the *Zero Day* feature).
- **Trademark "Portal Pals"** in the classes we'll need later so we never
  negotiate against ourselves: video games (9/41), toys/games (28),
  entertainment services (41), apparel (25), amusement park services (41).
  Clear the mark against existing registrations before falling in love.
- **Franchise bible v1** (`portalpals/WORLD.md`): the five Pals' kid
  designs, the mascot, Dorsey/Deb-bot, tone laws (funny, brave, zero gore,
  "misunderstood not monstrous"), and the canon database schema.
- **Win on its own:** an ownable, defensible IP position for legal fees
  and a writers'-room week.
- **Gate → Phase 2:** bible approved; marks cleared and filed; title chain
  clean.

### PHASE 2 — The game (the flagship market test)

Ship *Portal Pals* the video game — browser + mobile, free — through this
repo's Game Factory pipeline (concept doc → single-file build → headless
logic pass → art → instrumented funnel). The cheapest audience test a
franchise can buy.

- **Core loop:** rogue portals open across fun worlds; pick your Pal, use
  their verb (blast / smash-freeze / fly / portal-hop / hack-close) to
  round up runaway Parabots and seal the breach; befriend the good ones —
  your **Parabot collection** is the retention spine (collect, name, and
  house the bots you've rescued).
- **Five Pals = five mechanics** (see §0.5), so character preference is
  measurable: which Pal do kids pick, replay, share?
- **Cartridge power-ups** mirror the future toy line — the economy design
  and the merch line are the same design.
- **Instrument everything:** starts, D1/D7 return, session length,
  share/challenge rate, per-Pal picks. Character-affinity telemetry is the
  data no focus group can buy.
- **Win on its own:** a live, low-cost game earning attention and modest
  cosmetic revenue, profitable at web-game scale.
- **Gate → Phase 3:** directional — D1 ≥ 30%, D7 ≥ 10%, median session
  ≥ 6 min, share rate ≥ 3%, and a clear Pal-affinity signal. Miss =
  iterate the game, not escalate the spend.

### PHASE 3 — The content flywheel (animated shorts)

Weekly 60–90s animated shorts on YouTube/YouTube Kids, mirroring the
game's weekly content drop from the same canon database. The dueling-
narrators device from the script's opening is the house format: the Strong
Voice hypes it, the Nerdy Voice corrects it, the gag lands.

- Every short ends where the game begins: "A portal just opened in *your*
  browser — go seal it." Installs are the measurable click-through.
- Cheap 2D tier first; A/B the art style across the first batch and let
  watch-through pick the look the series inherits.
- **Win on its own:** a self-funding channel (ad revenue + UA value) and a
  subscriber base — a media asset even if nothing else follows.
- **Gate → Phase 4:** 50%+ average retention on shorts, 100k+ subscribers,
  measurable short→game lift, cumulative organic views in the tens of
  millions. This package *is* the series pitch.

### PHASE 4 — The series (someone else's balance sheet)

Pitch streamers/broadcast with what no spec pilot has: MAU, watch-through,
subscriber growth, and per-character affinity data proving which Pals kids
love. We license a proven audience, not a premise.

- Format: 11-minute episodes, two per block. Episode formula: a portal
  opens somewhere wild → the Pals deploy (LASS launch = the transformation
  ritual) → verb-driven caper → a Parabot is misunderstood, then befriended
  or a Dorsey scheme is foiled → home base at Charlene's.
- Every episode's portal world ships simultaneously as a playable game
  drop — the "watch it, then play it" ritual is the structural moat.
- **Win on its own:** streamer-financed, revenue-positive by construction;
  mass reach for the brand.
- **Gate → Phase 5:** season 1 completion metrics + season 2 renewal, and
  game MAU growth during the airing window (the flywheel must spin both
  ways).

### PHASE 5 — Merchandise & syndication (the profit engine)

Toys enter **after** kids know the characters — never before. The line is
pre-designed because the script designed it:

- **Wave 1:** five Pal figures with their signature action features +
  mascot plush + the **LASS playset** (land/air/sea/space modes) + a
  **Portal Playset** (figures go in one portal, pop out another — Wu's
  gimmick as a toy).
- **Wave 2:** **cartridge & orb accessory packs** (ice-web, power tubes,
  leap-frog orbs — straight from the script's canon), blind-box
  **Parabots** (the collect-and-befriend layer from the game), and a
  trading card game keyed to portal-world combos.
- **Toy↔game handshake:** every toy carries a code unlocking its in-game
  twin — connected play without hardware risk.
- Test SKUs in limited retail first; scale on sell-through, not hope.
- **Win on its own:** licensing minimum guarantees are cash-positive
  before broad shipment.
- **Gate → Phase 6:** test-market sell-through ≥ 65%, repeat purchase on
  blind-box/cartridge lines, series renewed.

### PHASE 6 — The films (two doors, one universe)

With a durable kids' brand established, the film play is dual-track:

1. **The Portal Pals animated feature** — the kids' event; premise from
   the mythology the series has been seeding (Max learns what the Key
   really is; both worlds at stake — the script's third act, kid-scaled:
   the algorithm saves the day and the nerd saves the soldier).
2. **ZERO DAY, the live-action/hybrid tentpole** — the original screenplay,
   PG-13, produced once the brand guarantees opening-weekend awareness.
   The grown-up audience is the kids who aged up plus their parents who
   sat through 200 shorts.

- Co-finance/partner at maximum leverage: audience data + merch run-rate
  mean we trade the least equity at this stage, not the most.
- **Win on its own:** either film is a marketing event that resets toy
  waves and game MAU even at box-office break-even.
- **Gate → Phase 7:** a demonstrated multi-year brand (3–5 years of
  sustained engagement), because parks underwrite decades.

### PHASE 7 — Location-based entertainment (the compounding asset)

Cheap-first, licensed always:

1. **Portal Pals: Portal Academy — traveling exhibit** for science centers
   and museums (portal physics, robotics, "build your Parabot friend" —
   the brand's curiosity spine makes this a natural, low-capex beachhead
   and a rolling billboard).
2. **The dark ride / land**, licensed to established operators: guests
   board a LASS, portal between worlds, and the finale is generated from
   choices made on-ride — seal the breach, rescue the Parabot. Each land
   expansion = a new portal world from the canon database.

- **Win on its own:** fee-based LBE licensing; the exhibit tour is
  near-term revenue.

## The connective tissue (why each phase validates the next)

1. **One canon database** — characters, cartridges, portal worlds, Parabot
   types — rendered by game, shorts, series, toys, and rides. New content
   ships everywhere at once for the cost of a data row.
2. **Telemetry before greenlights.** Never buy with projections what you
   can buy with data; the game and channel are the franchise's permanent
   focus group.
3. **Everything wins standalone.** Game profits at web scale; channel
   self-funds; series is streamer-financed; merch carries minimum
   guarantees; films are co-financed at peak leverage; parks are fee-based.
   No phase bets the franchise.
4. **The ritual moat:** watch the portal open, then go seal it yourself.
   Competitors can copy a character; they can't copy a loop that spans
   screen and game from one canon.
5. **"Misunderstood, not monstrous"** is the brand law — it's what turns a
   war story into a kids' franchise parents co-sign, and the parents'
   co-sign is the only durable distribution channel in kids' entertainment.
